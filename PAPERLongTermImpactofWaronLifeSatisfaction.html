The Long Term Impact of Experiencing War on Life Satisfaction - Evidence from the Life in Transition Survey

Author

Jemesa Landers, Tom Coupé, and Andrea Menclova

I. Introduction

There is a growing literature on the impact of experiencing war on life satisfaction ( see Coupe and Obrizan (2023) for a recent review). Interestingly, however, a number of papers in this literature come to opposite conclusions even though they use the same dataset, and the same variables to measure life satisfaction and to measure war.

Using the 2010 wave of the Life in Transition Survey (LITSII), which covers over 30000 respondents from thirty five European and Central Asian countries, Kijewskji (2020) finds that respondents who were physically injured, or had parents or grandparents physically injured or killed during the Second World War, have significantly lower levels of life satisfaction, measured on a scale between 1 and 10 (Table 4, −0.108, p<0.01, n=25618). She concludes ‘Our findings indicate that war experiences continue to be related to lower levels of life satisfaction even six decades after the end of the war’.

However, Ivlevs (2015), also using LITSII, the same war variable and the same life satisfaction variable (but restricting the sample to those less than 65 years old), finds a positive and significant effect (Table 2, 0.071, p<0.05, n=24070). He explains this positive effect by referring to the happiness of having survived, to war increasing “feelings of shared purpose, solidarity, trust, friendship and national pride”, and to pride of having “someone in their family died as a ‘martyr’ or was fighting for a ‘good’ or ‘holy’ cause’” (p. 349).

Finally, Child and Nikolova (2020) restricts the LITSII sample to “15 countries which experienced significant disruption from World War II battle activity” and finds no significant effect of the same war variable on the same life satisfaction variable (Table 8, –0.006, p>0.1, n=9538).

Noteworthy, both Kijewskji (2020) and Child and Nikolova (2020) (but not Ivlevs (2015)) include a second war-related variable, a dummy for whether the respondent, or any of his/her parents or grandparents were displaced during World War II, as control variable. Both estimated this variable to have a positive and significant effect on life satisfaction (Table 4, 0.118, p<0.01, n=25,618, and Table 8, 0.078, p<0.01, n=9538, respectively).

Besides these 3 studies, there are two other studies that use the same LITSII dataset but use slightly different measures of life satisfaction and/or war experience. Nikolova and Sanfey (2016) recode the 1 to 10 life satisfaction scale into a 1 to 5 scale, and merge both aspects (being displaced and/or injured/killed in the war) into one war experience variable, and find no significant effect (Table A1, 0.0241 , p>0.1, n=25666). In addition, they use a second life satisfaction question included in LITS II, which is also on a 1 to 5 scale, and again find no significant effect (Table A1, 0.00630, p>0.1, n=25427). Using the latter question and the merged war variable, Djankov and Zilinsky (2016) also find no effect (Table 5, −0.008, p>0.1, n=24836).

The above studies taken together make a ‘real-life’ ‘many analyst project’. In a ‘many analyst project’ (see for example, Huntington-Klein (2021), or in the context of life satisfaction Suzanne Hoogeveen (2021)), a researcher provides a large number of analysts with the same dataset and research question, and then analyzes how their conclusions differ. Typically, such projects document substantial differences in methodological choices and in some cases, substantial differences in conclusions. To show the range of possible outcomes (and avoid conflicting papers to be published), Simonsohn (2021) proposes specification curve analysis, which consists of conducting joint inference across all ‘theoretically relevant’ and ‘statistically valid’ specifications.

In this paper, we analyze how much we can learn from the LITSII survey about the long term impact of experiencing war on life satisfaction. We first replicate the main findings of the above 5 papers. Replication the papers will allow us to exclude that the opposing findings are the result of coding errors or typos in the published papers. Next, we use the specifications proposed by the 5 papers as the starting point of a specification curve analysis, allowing us to assess how strong the evidence is that the LITSII survey can provide about the long term impact of war on life satisfaction.

Note that, while the above studies focus on how World War II affects happiness, none of them allowed for other, more recent and local wars, to affect happiness, even though questions about victimization and displacement related tot these other wars are available in the LITSII survey. What’s more, Obrizan (2019), using the third wave of the Life in Transition survey (LITSIII), regresses life satisfaction (a happy/not happy dummy) on these recent war experiences, and finds insignificant negative effects of having injured or killed household members, but significant negative effects of having been displaced by these more recent and local wars (table 2, -0.063, p<0.01, n=18157). Interestingly, he did not allow for World War II experience to influence life satisfaction.1 In this paper, we will include all 5 war experiences available in LITSII in the analysis.

It is important to point out that it is hard to obtain causal evidence based on non-experimental survey data as the ‘treatment’ variable is not randomized. At the same time, it is hard to see how one could ethically run an experiment to measure the long term effect of experiencing war on life satisfaction. Within the constraints of using survey data, the LITS survey has a number of important advantages.The survey is large, with more than 30000 respondents. It has two questions that measure satisfaction with life. Finally, while few large scale surveys ask about war experiences, LITS has 5 questions related to war experience. Hence, among what is available, LITS is among the best possible sources to estimate the long term impact of war on life satisfaction.

The remainder of the paper is structured as follows. Section II replicates the 5 papers that provide estimates of the impact of World War II on life satisfaction. Section III provides a specification curve analysis. Section IV concludes.

II. Replicating the literature

Given the ample evidence of the replication crisis in the social sciences (Baker (2016), Reed (2018)), we want to make sure the conflicting findings reported by the various papers are not the results of typos in the published versions or errors in the computer codes used to generate these findings. We therefor replicate the key regression of these papers by following the descriptions of the method, included variables and construction of the variables provided in the papers.

Table 2 shows that the replicated results for Kijewskji (2020), Nikolova and Sanfey (2016) and Djankov and Zilinsky (2016) are almost identical to the published results. Like Child and Nikolova (2020) we find an insignificant effect but the replicated effect size is considerably more negative. Like Ivlevs (2015) we find a positive estimate. Summarizing, our replication of the literature estimating the impact of experiencing war on life satisfaction, tends to give somewhat more weight towards finding a negative effect and casts some doubt on the possibility of their being a positive effect.

III. A Specification Curve Analysis

To further explore the reasons why different authors found different estimates, we next do a specification curve or multiverse analysis (Simonsohn (2021), Steegen and Vanpaemel (2016)).

The papers we replicated all used the same dataset. But the authors used this same dataset in many different ways2. As explained in the introduction, there were differences in what measure of life satisfaction was selected (1-5 scale versus 1-10 scale) and in how the war variable was included (only the variable reflecting injuries/death was include, the displacement-by-war variable was also included, or both variables were aggregated).

Some of the control variables were common to all studies. All studies included variables capturing the age, gender, employment status, marital status and health status of the respondent. They also included measures of the education of the respondent and the respondent’s father. All but Kijewskji (2020) included income as a control variable. But there were small differences in how they included these variables: for example, some included dummies for all categories of education while some aggregated some categories of education into wider categories.

But besides the common set of control variables, there was lots of idiosyncratic variable choices. Kijewskji (2020) controled for differences in occupations, religious and civic involvment. Child and Nikolova (2020) controled for mother’s education, whether the respondent or family members had been member of the communist party, whether the respondent had ever moved, and for rural/urban differences. Ivlevs (2015) controled for minority status, urban/rural status, the number of children in the household, the existence of a migration network, differences in assets and differences in financial satisfaction.

The authors also chose different ways to capture geographic differences. Some included country fixed effects (Ivlevs (2015), Nikolova and Sanfey (2016)), others for region specific fixed effects (Child and Nikolova (2020)) while yet others included country random effects in addtion to country level characteristics like unemployment or per capita GDP (Kijewskji (2020)), or included just country level characteristics such as whether the country was a transition country (Djankov and Zilinsky (2016)).

Studies differed on whether or not they clustered standard errors and in whether or not they weighed observations. Kijewskji (2020) did not cluster standard errors or use survey weights. All others used within country population weights and clustered standard errors at the level of country, region or locality.

Finally, studies differed in which observations they included: Ivlevs (2015) focuses on those who are less than 65 years old, Kijewskji (2020) excludes Kosovo, while Child and Nikolova (2020) focus on 15 countries that were affected by World War II.

To illustrate the impact of methodological differences, Simonsohn (2021) suggest to start from the ‘set of theoretically justified, statistically valid, and non-redundant specifications’. In this study we use the specifications chosen by the 5 papers as the starting point for our set. While not necessarily exhaustive, given these specifications all have been deemed acceptable by these authors, the referees that reviewed these papers and the editors that decided to accept these papers for publication, the specifications all can be considered ‘reasonable’.

More specifically, we use the 5 weight, clustering and fixed effect choices of the papers. But vary what dependent variable (1-5 scale versus 1-10 scale) is used, and what sub-population is used (15 heavily affected countries only, only under 65 year olds, or a combination of these two). We include the common control variables in all specifications3 but vary the set of additional controls (include income, include all other controls used by any study, include additional war variables, and combination of the aforementioned variable (sets)).

Figure 1 displays the specification curve and allows us to identify consequential specification choices.

We run a total of 320 specifications, with panel A showing the estimated effect sizes (and their confidence intervals) of the variables reflecting whether the respondent, or a family member, was injured or killed during World War II. Estimates are ordered from small to big, with statistically significant estimates being highlighted in red (if negative) or blue (if positive). About 90% of these estimates are negative and the median estimate is -0.05. A substantial part of the negative estimates are statistically significant (37.5%) but none of the positive estimates are statistically significant.

Figure I: A Specification Curve for the impact of being injured/killed relatives during World War 2 on Life Satisfaction

Figure II shows how estimates differ across models, control variables included, sample restrictions and dependent variables. The biggest influence comes from including income as a control variable in the regression. Omitting the income variable drives the estimated coefficient of the injured/killed war variable downwards and increases the chance the estimate is statistically significant. In fact, none of the estimated effects is statistically significant if income is included as a control variable. This is noteworthy as Kijewskji (2020) is the only study that does not include the income variable as a control and is the only study that finds a significant negative effect. There are good reasons to include income as a control variable as income is typically included as a variable in life satisfaction regressions, allowing for money to ‘buy’ happiness. At the same time, one could argue that income is a ‘bad’ control: if the war variable affects income, controlling for income will obscure some of the effect of the war on life satisfaction. That being said, all 5 papers included health as a control variable, and like for income one could come up with a justification for why war experience should affect current health status, and hence, why health would be a bad control.

Figure II: What affects the estimated effect size of being injured/killed relatives during World War 2

Other factors matter much less: a regression of estimated effect size on the characteristic of the specification show that, keeping everything else constant, the model by Djankov and Zilinsky (2016) has somewhat maller effect sizes than the models by Ivlevs (2015), Kijewskji (2020) and Nikolova and Sanfey (2016). Similarly, using the 1-10 life satisfaction scale results is somewhat smaller estimates, while differences in who is included in the sample do not seem to affect the effect sizes.

III. What about other war variables

So far we have focused on the impact of casualties of World War 2. Now we focus on what affects the effect size of the variables that reflects whether or not the respondent’s family moved because of World War 2.

Interestingly, Figure 3 shows the effect of having moved to be mostly positive (median=0.07,90% positive, majority significantly so).

Figure III: A Specification Curve for the impact of having because of World War 2 on Life Satisfaction

But we again find one factor that crucially affects conclusions: if one uses the 1-5 life satisfaction scale one finds much smaller and rarely significant effect sizes, while most specifications using the 1-10 life satisfaction scale find a positive and significant effect.

Figure IV: What affects the estimated effect size of having moved because of World War 2 on Life Satisfaction

Finally, we also explored the specification curves for the 3 recent war variables: whether one was injured in a recent conflict, whether one moved because of a recent conflict and whether one has family members injured or killed because of a recent conflict. The effect size of all these variables varied a lot but few specifications showed significant effects.

IV. Conclusions

In this paper, we replicate the literature that uses the second wave of the LIFE in Transition Survey (LITSII) to estimate the impact of war on life satisfaction and conduct a specification curve analysis. While war experience is not fully randomized, limiting causal claims one can make about its impact, the LITS II survey is a great source of observational data to investigate the relationship between war and life satisfaction given it asked a large sample of respondents about various war experiences and measures life satisfactions in multiple ways.

Given the suitability of the LITS survey, it is no surprise that the published literature ( Kijewskji (2020), Child and Nikolova (2020), Ivlevs (2015), Nikolova and Sanfey (2016), Djankov and Zilinsky (2016)) using LITSII to regress life satisfaction on war experience is sizeable. More surprising is that, depending on the paper one reads, one can find a significant negative effect, a significant positive effect or insignificant effects of war experience on life satisfaction, despite these studies all using the same datasets and similar measures of life satisfaction and war experience.

In this paper, we explore the reasons for this heterogeneity in findings. Replicating the 5 papers, we find effect sizes that are similar to those published. However, we do not find that the estimated positive effect from the literature remains significant when replicated. A specification curve analysis further finds that, based on 320 ‘reasonable’ specifications, the impact of being injured/having a family member being injured or killed during World War 2 is mainly estimated to be negative but that these estimates are statistically significant only when not including income as a control variable. We further show that the impact of having moved because of World War 2 is mainly estimated to have a positive effect but only so when measuring life satisfaction on a 1-10 scale (rather than a 1-5 scale). Finally, we find little support for a significant effect of recent war experience on life satisfaction.

Overall, this case study shows that the choices researchers make when analyzing a dataset can have a major impact on the conclusions they draw. It also should be a warning to readers that single papers, even when including several robustness checks, do not necessarily represent well what one would learn from a more thorough analysis from the same dataset. Finally, the analysis here suggests a comprehensive specification curve analysis should be a part of any empirical paper to give the reader a much better idea of the true extent of model uncertainty.
